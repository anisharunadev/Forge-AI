# PRD Quality Review — Forge Delivery Accelerator

## Overall verdict

This is a structurally disciplined PRD with a clear thesis (delivery quality as a property of the system, not the individual), a defensible North Star (TTTD), a credible persona prioritization, and an unusually honest separation of pilot targets from numeric commitments. The seven-dimension walk surfaces a serious downstream blocker: the functional requirements are a capability inventory, not testable specifications — "Done" is not defined for any FR, and the entire PRD leans on the Pilot + counter-metrics machinery to defer that work. That is a legitimate strategy for this stage, but it means downstream story creation will need to re-engineer acceptance criteria from scratch. The PRD is decision-ready for an investment/steering decision; it is not yet ready to feed a sprint without substantial FR-level tightening.

## Decision-readiness — adequate

The PRD does a credible job of surfacing real trade-offs rather than smoothing them. TTTD was chosen over Delivery Predictability explicitly because "predictability is a second-order outcome leadership cannot see daily" (PRD §2.1, decision log entry "Vision override"); the rejection is recorded in addendum §B.1. Brownfield-first is locked as DL-001 with the same rigor. The Tech Lead elevation from "project onboarding" to delivery ownership is named as a deliberate reframing (decision log) and survives the user-steering iterations.

The Open Questions section is honest about phase-blockers (OQ-001 through OQ-008) and assigns owners and resolution paths. Counter-metrics are named (PRD §2.4) and tied to the threats they catch (gate-skip, override spike). The "what Forge is not" section (§1.6) reads as earned leadership reassurance, not boilerplate — it cites the NFR that enforces each claim (NFR-032, F-205 + F-006, etc.).

The weak point: the Open Questions table references "Section 2.5" in OQ-003, but Section 2 has no §2.5 — the table is at §2.4. Also, `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts are not present at all, even at real tensions (e.g., the A-004 hierarchy reversal, the relaxed 100+ workflow target, the F-209 context-aware claim that "MUST" consume three inputs without a measurable differentiator). The PRD relies on the decision log and addendum to carry the same load that `[NOTE FOR PM]` tags usually carry.

### Findings
- **medium** Section 2.5 phantom reference (PRD §6.1 OQ-003) — OQ-003 says "Per Section 2.5, no numbers committed until baseline exists," but Section 2 has subsections 2.1–2.4 only; the reference target is the §2.4 counter-metrics table or an earlier draft. *Fix:* correct the cross-reference to "§2.4" or "§2 (post-pilot commitment posture)."
- **medium** A-004 hierarchy reversal is buried in §6.2 (PRD §6.2 A-004) — the change from "Project = Tenant" to "Tenant = Customer Engagement, Projects exist within a tenant" cascades through the entire multi-tenancy model (NFR-006, F-004, addendum §A.1, decision log) and is exactly the kind of reversal a PM would want surfaced as a `[NOTE FOR PM]` at §3 (Personas), §4 (Capability Groups), and §5.2. *Fix:* add `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts at the three load-bearing sections pointing to A-004.
- **low** NFR-009 100+ concurrent workflows relaxation is under-celebrated (PRD §5.3) — the earlier 1000+ target was rejected and the new target is a defensible V1 number, but the PRD treats it as a routine NFR entry. The addendum §B.7 captures the rejection. *Fix:* add a one-line rationale in the §5.3 row explaining "V1 users are KnackForge internal, not 10,000 enterprise users" to prevent next-pass authors from re-relaxing the target.

## Substance over theater — adequate

The Vision statement (§1.1) is specific to KnackForge, names the artifact model (Org Knowledge + Project Intelligence + governed workflows), and explains the move from "individual expertise" to "system property." It is not the kind of statement that could swap into any PRD in this category. The "Why now" (§1.5) is an earned insight — AI coding tools commoditized code generation, so the differentiator becomes the delivery system. That is a real bet, not boilerplate.

Personas are well-prioritized. The Tech Lead is named primary with an explicit JBTD, and the persona inventory resists the "more personas = thorough PRD" trap by listing out-of-scope personas (AI Delivery Operator, Replacement Engineer, Autonomous Release Manager) and future-state personas. The Delivery Sponsor addition is a deliberate response to "funders and success-measurers belong in the persona inventory" (decision log).

NFR theater is mostly avoided. Most NFRs have product-specific thresholds (NFR-014 RPO/RTO, NFR-027 3–5 engineer-days connector target, NFR-009 100+ concurrent workflows) or honest `[TO BE MEASURED]` placeholders. The exceptions are NFR-001 (SOC2-ready without certification) and NFR-002 (GDPR-ready without DPA), which are status-as-requirement statements; they earn their place because the addendum §B.6 records the rejection of premature commitment, but the language could be tighter.

Innovation theater is the cleanest section. The PRD explicitly disclaims "not a coding-agent bake-off" (§1.6, decision log "Vision override") rather than constructing a fake differentiation matrix. Differentiation rests on F-209 (context-aware architecture generation), F-103/F-110 (project intelligence + impact analysis), and NFR-032 (human approval gates) — claims with concrete FRs behind them.

### Findings
- **low** "Delivery quality as a property of the system" appears as a leadership phrase (PRD §1.2, §1.3) but the PRD never operationalizes it — no NFR, no FR, no SM measures "system property" directly. Architecture Drift Rate (§2.3) and Human Override Rate (§2.4) are the closest proxies. *Fix:* either add a one-line explanation in §2.3 mapping "system property" to Architecture Drift Rate + Human Override Rate, or accept the leadership framing as branding.
- **low** Vision is a system product; "delivery intelligence" is a brand term used 8+ times across the PRD (PRD frontmatter, §1.1, §1.3, §1.4 pillar 3) without a tight definition. Pillar 3's definition ("Every engagement contributes reusable knowledge that accelerates future work") reads as a category, not a capability. *Fix:* tighten the pillar 3 definition in §1.4 or accept it as an umbrella.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The PRD has a thesis and the features serve it. The thesis is "delivery quality as a property of the system" and the three pillars (Codified Methodology, Knowledge Propagation, Delivery Intelligence) are each grounded in concrete capabilities: F-001–F-008 (Foundation) map to pillar 1; F-201–F-210 (Architecture) and the approval chain map to pillar 2; F-101–F-111 (Project Intelligence) and the knowledge graph map to pillar 3. The MVP scope kind is clear: problem-solving MVP (understand the brownfield project, then generate a governed architecture package), not experience MVP and not platform MVP.

Feature prioritization follows the thesis. Foundation before Phase 0 before Phase 1 is justified (§8.1) on substrate dependency. The "Practice Standard" stage between Multi-Engagement and Commercial (§8.3) is a thoughtful addition that addresses the "we use it ourselves" leadership framing without a commercial commitment. The P1.5 Architecture Validation Gate (§8.2) is the kind of discipline that prevents spending 8 weeks measuring the wrong system.

Success metrics validate the thesis: TTTD (North Star), Architecture Drift Rate (the "system property" claim), Human Override Rate (trust), Knowledge Reuse Rate (knowledge layer working). Counter-metrics are anti-gaming instruments that catch the obvious failure modes (gate-skip, blanket-waiver, autonomy creep). Adoption signals (§8.5) include Artifact Consumption Rate (utility, not just generation) and Self-Reported Time Saved (qualitative signal before hard ROI), which is the right read of V1 maturity.

The single strategic weak point is the absence of a "what we are not building" story for the architecture model itself. The PRD commits to multi-tenancy, shared org knowledge, per-tenant project intelligence, human approval gates, model provider independence, cost governance, knowledge freshness, and SOC2-ready controls (§5.11) — eight foundational constraints. These are listed but never prioritized or sequenced. Architecture will need to read this list and decide which constraints dominate when they conflict (e.g., cost governance vs. knowledge freshness — can the system budget for freshness?).

### Findings
- **medium** §5.11 foundational constraints are unranked (PRD §5.11) — eight constraints, all marked as "fixed decisions," but no ordering, no conflict resolution policy. When NFR-030 (cost governance) and NFR-031 (knowledge freshness) conflict (always-fresh indexing is expensive), what wins? *Fix:* add a one-paragraph precedence or conflict-resolution policy, or add a `[NOTE FOR PM]` flagging that constraint conflicts are an Architecture ADR.
- **low** "Practice Standard" stage is well-motivated but unmeasurable (PRD §8.3) — the exit criterion for moving to Strategic Phase B is implicit ("KnackForge uses Forge as the default delivery workflow"). No threshold for what "default" means (e.g., 80% of new engagements use Forge for governed architecture? All new engagements?). *Fix:* add an exit criterion to §8.3 or add it to the §8.4 validation timing table.

## Done-ness clarity — thin

This is the dimension the PRD leans hardest on the pilot to defer. No FR has a testable acceptance criterion embedded in its description. The FRs read as capability names: "F-101 Repository Ingestion — Pull repositories from connected source-control systems. Idempotent re-ingest. Honors rate limits." The "idempotent" and "honors rate limits" are properties, not tests. A story author would need to define: what does "idempotent re-ingest" look like in test code? What's the rate-limit handling contract for GitHub (5000 req/hr) vs. Bitbucket vs. Azure DevOps?

F-209 (Context-Aware Architecture Generation) is the strongest claim in the PRD — "MUST consume requirement + project knowledge graph + applicable standards" — and is named as "the key differentiator" (§4.3). Yet there is no acceptance criterion for "context-aware": no metric for how much of the generated ADR traces to each input source, no threshold for what counts as context-aware vs. context-influenced, no definition of "consume" (RAG retrieval, in-context tokens, structured input assembly). Architecture will define this; story creation will define this; but the PRD names it as a differentiator without defining it.

The NFRs that do carry thresholds are good: NFR-009 (100+ concurrent), NFR-010 (10–20 repos in 24h), NFR-013 (99.9% availability), NFR-014 (RPO ≤ 24h, RTO ≤ 4h), NFR-027 (3–5 engineer-days per connector). NFR-011 and NFR-012 (impact analysis latency, approval latency) are honest `[TO BE MEASURED]` placeholders, which is correct for V1 but means the PRD is silent on whether 5-second approval latency is acceptable.

The NFRs that drift toward adjectives: NFR-015 "Brownfield graceful degradation" and NFR-033 "Partial Discovery Tolerance" overlap conceptually and use "graceful" / "tolerable" / "remains usable" language without thresholds. The PRD does not define "partially stale" or "unavailable" quantitatively.

### Findings
- **critical** F-209 (Context-Aware Architecture Generation) is a named differentiator with no testable definition (PRD §4.3, §1.6) — "MUST consume requirement + project knowledge graph + applicable standards" is a property statement, not a test. Story creation cannot write an acceptance test for "context-aware" without inventing the contract. *Fix:* add 1–2 lines defining what counts as a context-aware output (e.g., "Generated ADR includes a `context_sources` field listing the specific project intelligence nodes and standard IDs that informed each decision; the Steward can audit which sources the Architect saw vs. which were retrieved") or add to OQ-006 / OQ-007 as a phase-blocker.
- **high** FR-level acceptance criteria are absent (PRD §4.1–§4.3) — every FR is a capability statement. 30+ FRs, none with embedded acceptance. The pilot phasing (§8.2) and counter-metrics (§2.4) defer this to pilot execution, but that means story creation has no source material. *Fix:* add an "Acceptance Notes" column to the §4 capability tables (or a new §4.5 "Acceptance Sketch" subsection) with one measurable outcome per FR — even a placeholder.
- **high** NFR-015 and NFR-033 overlap and use "graceful" / "tolerable" / "usable" without thresholds (PRD §5.4) — two NFRs covering brownfield failure modes with no defined threshold for "partially stale" or "remains usable." *Fix:* merge into one NFR with a quantitative threshold (e.g., "System continues to return answers with explicit staleness flags when up to 30% of connected sources are unreachable; queries with no reachable source return an error, not a fabricated answer") and reference NFR-031 for the freshness signal.
- **medium** F-110 (Impact Analysis) is the "most impressive demo feature" (PRD §4.2) with no correctness definition — what makes an impact analysis "correct"? % recall (true affected services identified) vs. % precision (no false positives)? Without this, P1.5's "≥80% accepted without major correction" exit criterion (§8.2) is the only correctness signal and is post-hoc. *Fix:* add a forward-looking definition (e.g., "Recall target ≥ X% on a curated benchmark; precision measured against Architect review").

## Scope honesty — strong

The PRD is unusually honest about omissions. The non-goals are explicit (§6.3): no replacement of engineers, no autonomous delivery, no replacement of systems of record, no 100% automation, no bake-off against coding agents, no production code without human review, no BYOK at V1, no multi-region at V1, no custom customer methodologies, no multi-org federation, no autonomous repo modification, no autonomous production deploy. Each is enforced by a named NFR or section.

Out-of-V1 capability phases (§4.4) and out-of-V1 NFRs (§5.10) are listed explicitly. The "Why this order" reasoning in §8.1 is the rare PRD that explains the V1 cut. A-004 (tenant = engagement) is recorded as an explicit refinement from a wrong earlier draft — exactly the kind of reversal a downstream reader needs to see.

The minor weaknesses: A-001 through A-008 are listed in §6.2 but not indexed at the end of the document (the rubric asks for an Assumptions Index roundtrip). The inline `[ASSUMPTION: …]` tags are not used; assumptions live in the §6.2 table. That's a deliberate structural choice and arguably cleaner than inline tags, but the rubric flags the roundtrip as a downstream concern.

### Findings
- **medium** Open-items density is high on a green-light-to-build PRD (PRD §6.1, §6.2) — 8 phase-blockers + 8 assumptions + 6 non-blockers = 22 open items. For an investment/steering decision this is appropriate; for a sprint start, this is a blocker. The PRD is honest about this (each row has owner + resolution path), but the count deserves a one-line callout so downstream readers don't underestimate the follow-up work. *Fix:* add a count or summary at the top of §6 ("22 open items: 8 phase-blockers, 8 assumptions, 6 deferred") to make the density visible.
- **low** A-008 (organization knowledge curated manually) is load-bearing and lives only in §6.2 (PRD §6.2, addendum §A.3) — this assumption means the Steward is the source of all standards and templates, and it directly contradicts the "Delivery Intelligence" pillar framing (§1.4) which says "every engagement contributes reusable knowledge." If engagements contribute, who's curating? *Fix:* add a one-line callout in §1.4 noting that pillar 3 is an umbrella covering future-state contributions; V1 contributions come from the Steward, not the engagement.

## Downstream usability — adequate

The PRD has the structures a downstream workflow needs: globally numbered FRs (F-001 to F-210), NFRs (NFR-001 to NFR-033), Open Questions (OQ-001 to OQ-008), Assumptions (A-001 to A-008), Non-blockers (N-001 to N-006), Decision Log (DL-001 to DL-011). Cross-references are mostly resolvable: NFR-032 → F-205 + F-006, F-001 → F-002 dependencies, Phase 0 outputs to Phase 1 inputs, etc.

The glossary is implicit, not explicit. Domain nouns — "Engagement," "Tenant," "Project," "Knowledge Graph," "ADR," "API Contract," "Standards Attestation" — are used across FRs, UJs (if present), and SM definitions without a glossary section. The addendum §A provides a domain model but does not function as a glossary. "Engagement" and "Tenant" are defined as synonyms in A-004, but a story author who reads only the PRD will not find this definition in a single searchable place.

UJ protagonists: there are no UJ sections in the PRD. The rubric says UJs are load-bearing for consumer products and multi-stakeholder B2B (which Forge is) and may be overhead for single-operator internal tools. Forge is multi-stakeholder and capability-first, so the absence of UJs is a deliberate shape choice. The PRD instead has JBTDs in §3.1 and inline persona references throughout §4. That's defensible for this product shape, but the persona-thesis through-line (Tech Lead as primary) would be more legible as 2–3 named UJs carrying the through-line end-to-end (e.g., "Onboard a new Tech Lead to a brownfield project," "Take an approved requirement through the architecture gate"). Story creation will likely re-invent these UJs from §8.6's demo script — which is itself a UJ in narrative form, just not labeled as one.

The §8.6 Demo Path is unusually valuable as a downstream input. It is a 9-step scenario with named artifacts, named connectors, and a target runtime. It functions as an integration test specification for the whole PRD. Architecture can use it as a smoke test; UX can use it as the canonical happy path; story creation can use it as a UJ.

### Findings
- **high** No glossary section; domain nouns drift across the PRD (PRD entire) — "Tenant" / "Engagement" defined as synonyms in A-004, "Knowledge Graph" / "Project Intelligence" / "Delivery Intelligence" used loosely, "ADR" / "ADR Generation" / "ADR (draft)" / "ADR (approved)" not distinguished. Story creation will need a glossary pass. *Fix:* add a "Glossary" section (even 8–10 entries) before §4 or after §6 with the canonical definitions.
- **medium** UJs are absent but the rubric notes this is a deliberate shape choice for capability-first multi-stakeholder products (PRD §3) — the PRD substitutes JBTDs in §3.1 and inline persona references. The §8.6 demo script is a UJ in narrative form. The trade-off is documented but not labeled. *Fix:* add a one-line note in §3 stating that UJs are intentionally omitted in favor of capability-first structure + the §8.6 demo as integration scenario, so downstream UX/story workflows know the convention.
- **medium** Addendum domain model (addendum §A) is not referenced in PRD body — A.1, A.2, A.3 are in the addendum, but FRs and NFRs reference "engagement," "project," "tenant" as if the model is settled. A reader of the PRD alone does not encounter the model. *Fix:* add a one-paragraph pointer in §5.2 (multi-tenancy) or §3.4 (systems of record) noting "see addendum §A for the canonical domain model."

## Shape fit — strong

The PRD is shaped for what it is: a strategic internal investment with optional commercial future, capability-first, multi-stakeholder, governance-heavy, multi-tenant, brownfield-first. The shape signals: capabilities grouped by phase (§4.1–§4.3), FR numbering global across the PRD (F-001 to F-210), personas limited to 4 V1-active (§3.1) with future-state and out-of-scope sections, NFRs grouped by domain, foundational architecture constraints surfaced as a separate section (§5.11), and a forward-flowing decision log (§7).

The capability-first shape matches the brief and the decision log ("Capability-first aligns to how the brief is organized and how a TL/Architect will reason about it"). The 8-milestone phasing (§8.1) is a roadmap, not a sprint plan — correct for this stage. The pilot phasing (§8.2) is a full pilot design with P0–P4 stages and explicit exit criteria. The rollout diagram (§8.3) is a four-stage flow with the right "Practice Standard" intermediate stage.

The one shape mismatch: §3.1 says "Forge is capability-first and multi-stakeholder" and lists 4 V1-active personas, but the PRD never resolves how a single capability group (e.g., Phase 0) gets evaluated against multiple personas. The §4 capability tables have a "Personas" column that lists 1–2 personas per FR, but the validation criteria (e.g., §8.5 adoption signals) are persona-agnostic. A multi-stakeholder B2B product usually has role-specific success criteria (Steward success ≠ Tech Lead success ≠ Architect success). The PRD has them implicitly but not explicitly.

### Findings
- **low** Persona-specific success criteria are implicit (PRD §3, §8.5) — the §8.5 adoption signals are all Tech Lead-centric (Active Tech Leads, Knowledge Reuse, First Aha Time). Steward success ("time to publish a new standard end-to-end") and Architect success ("approval cycle completion rate within SLA") are mentioned in passing but not surfaced as separate signals. *Fix:* add a row in §8.5 for each V1-active persona's primary signal, or note that the §8.5 signals are the unified adoption instrument.
- **low** §1.6 is a leadership reassurance section that exceeds the PRD's typical register (PRD §1.6) — the "what Forge is not" bullets are a register shift from the rest of the PRD. The decision log notes "Epics, UX, and Architecture artifacts should preserve it, not silently reduce it to a prohibition." That's correct guidance but the rest of the PRD is a calmer tone. *Fix:* keep as-is, but the §1.6 callout is the kind of content that needs to survive downstream compaction — consider moving the "Epics, UX, Architecture must preserve this register" sentence to the decision log so it doesn't get lost in editorial passes.

This is appropriate for a steering/investment decision (where the open items are commitments the steering body is buying into) and is a blocker for a sprint start. The PRD does not declare which stage it is in; the status field says "draft" but the body reads as if it is presented for sign-off.
